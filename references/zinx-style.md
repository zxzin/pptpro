# PPTPRO Zinx Presentation Style

Use this reference when the user asks PPTPRO for Zinx style, `Taste is all you need`, liquid glass, pixel particles, game-like HUD grammar, a short-video deck, or the red/yellow/blue/green Zinx palette. This is presentation art direction; PPTPRO's narrative, evidence, editability, script, render, and audit rules remain mandatory.

## Core Position

Zinx presentation style is a liquid-glass pixel-particle system built around visible human judgment.

- Liquid glass is the material layer: translucent light surfaces, controlled blur or transparency, quiet rims, and subtle depth.
- Pixel is the information grammar: HUD labels, segmented meters, page counters, square state tokens, sprite-like icons, and game feedback.
- Particles are atmosphere: tiny pixel dust or data motes behind content, never the message.
- Copy is part of the style: direct, specific, and free of template rhetoric.
- One slide carries one beat. Treat the sequence as levels with setup, tension, evidence, judgment, and completion.
- Static composition must work before any transition or animation is considered.

Do not imitate Zinx style with a logo, a few random particles, or four pasted color bars. The material, palette roles, component grammar, copy, and sequence must work together.

## Palette Lock

Use these exact base values unless the user supplies another Zinx palette:

```text
ink     #171512
white   #FFFFFF
paper   #F7FAFF
red     #E7372F
yellow  #FFD33D
blue    #2477FF
green   #16B35C
```

Assign deliberate roles:

- Red: alert, conflict, critique, or the strongest editorial beat.
- Yellow: source, idea, attention, or pending judgment.
- Blue: system, analysis, navigation, or selection.
- Green: completion, approval, output, or success.
- White/light paper: primary surface.
- Ink: text and compact interface structure.

All four role colors should appear across a full Zinx deck when the content supports them. Do not collapse the result into a blue-green duotone, rainbow decoration, or a dark neon technology theme.

## Material And Frame

- Use white or very light cool backgrounds.
- Build major panels as calm Mac-like glass shells with translucent white fill, a one-pixel light rim, subtle lower-edge shade, and low-opacity depth.
- Keep major panel radii consistent and moderate. Use pill shapes only for compact chips and controls.
- Use red/yellow/blue/green as clean flat fills, restrained refraction, state color, or compact tokens.
- Place tiny square particles behind content. Keep them sparse enough that text and evidence remain dominant.
- Prefer actual square pixels or 2-4 px clusters. Avoid glow blobs, bokeh, starfields, line textures, grids, scanlines, glossy streaks, and pasted highlight spots.
- Avoid thick black comic frames, heavy black offset shadows, colored cushion shadows, corner rails, and side stripes.
- Do not put large glass cards inside other glass cards. Use one shell, then organize internal content through spacing, rules with meaning, and compact labels.
- In PowerPoint, simulate glass with native transparency, light borders, restrained shadow, and layered shapes only when the result remains editable and renders consistently. A solid light fallback is better than broken fake glass.

## Interface Grammar

Use pixel details inside information components:

- **HUD chip:** compact status such as `SOURCE 02`, `CORE`, `SHELL`, `PICK`, or `DONE`.
- **Archive card:** a source or evidence object with a small source chip, readable body, and dominant artifact image.
- **Shell case:** before/after or core/shell comparison where the artifact remains the focal point.
- **Timeline node:** a real sequence with time, state, and segmented progress.
- **Reward card:** completion, selected option, final output, or publish result.
- **Meter/stat:** segmented bars and square state tokens that encode a judgment or measure.
- **Control glyph:** a familiar symbol or small pixel marker, not a decorative icon tile.

Do not repeat every component on every slide. Select the grammar that explains the current beat.

## Typography And Copy

- Use a readable system sans-serif for titles and body copy.
- Reserve pixel type for HUD labels, counters, compact buttons, and game cues.
- Keep the main theme literal and memorable. Chinese copy should sharpen the theme rather than translate it into a long subtitle.
- Prefer nouns, direct claims, causal statements, and named defects.
- Apply all banned-copy rules from `anti-ai-design.md`.
- Do not use active task-command headings or generic slogans as presentation titles.
- Keep detail in the speaker script. Slides carry structure, evidence, and rhythm.

## Slide Pacing

Adapt this sequence to the source instead of forcing every deck to use all stages:

1. **Title:** literal theme, one sharp supporting phrase, and a strong preview object.
2. **Choice/test:** two products, pages, objects, or directions that make judgment visible.
3. **Source anchor:** one or two credible sources shown as objects.
4. **Design judgment:** turn abstraction into a meter, diagram, comparison, or case.
5. **Shell case:** before/after or core/shell explanation with the artifact dominant.
6. **Life/use scene:** concrete awkward versus smooth experience.
7. **Boss/critique:** name the defect without making the warning visually chaotic.
8. **Structure:** show how language, code, rhythm, or system choices create the result.
9. **Application:** recording plan, workflow, decision, or output.
10. **Clear:** compact conclusion or completion state before the required PPTPRO closing slide.

For conventional academic or business decks, borrow the material and component grammar without forcing game terminology into visible copy.

## Signature

When no client or strict brand system governs the deck, a tiny `ZINX` or `ZX` mark may appear as quiet authorship.

- Place it at a bottom-right edge, metadata rail, footer token, or hidden corner.
- Keep it small, low-contrast, and secondary.
- Never present it as a feature, explain it in the deck, mention it in promotional copy, or create a slide about it.
- Do not let it be mistaken for the client's official logo.
- Omit it for white-label, legal/compliance, strict brand, accessibility, or clean-export requirements.

## Zinx QA

Before handoff:

- Confirm the slide still works with particles and decorative effects removed.
- Confirm glass is restrained and readable rather than a generic glassmorphism card pile.
- Confirm red, yellow, blue, and green have semantic roles rather than decorative placement.
- Confirm pixel elements encode state, sequence, measure, source, or feedback.
- Confirm no thick comic borders, cushion shadows, grids, stripes, scanlines, glow fields, or random particle noise remain.
- Confirm every portrait keeps the face visible after cropping.
- Confirm the source, product, figure, or case remains more prominent than the Zinx treatment.
- Confirm any `ZINX`/`ZX` mark stays hidden and unexplained.
- Render and inspect the cover, one source/evidence slide, the densest slide, the critique/boss beat when present, every portrait slide, the clear screen, and the final closing slide.
